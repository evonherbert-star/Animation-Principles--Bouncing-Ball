# Animation-Principles--Bouncing-Ball
This project studies the 12 principles of animation using a bouncing ball. By analyzing its motion—falling, impact, and rebound—it shows how timing, squash and stretch, arcs, and anticipation create realistic movement. It highlights how simple actions can effectively communicate weight, energy, and visual appeal.
Objective

The goal of this project is to understand how motion alone—without a character—can effectively communicate:

Weight

Timing

Energy

Realism

🧠 The 12 Principles in Action

1. Squash and Stretch
The ball compresses (squashes) when it hits the ground and stretches slightly as it moves upward. This shows flexibility and impact.

2. Timing
The speed of the ball changes naturally—fast when falling, slow at the peak. This reflects gravity and weight.

3. Anticipation
Before bouncing upward, the ball slightly compresses further at impact, preparing the viewer for the next motion.

4. Arcs
The ball follows a curved path rather than a straight line, creating smooth and natural movement.

5. Slow In and Slow Out
The ball slows down at the top of each bounce and accelerates as it falls, making the motion more realistic.

6. Follow Through and Overlapping Action
After impact, the ball may slightly wobble or settle, especially if it's soft.

7. Secondary Action
Additional motion like slight spinning or vibration enhances the main bounce.

8. Staging
The movement is clearly presented against a simple background, making the action easy to follow.

9. Straight Ahead Action and Pose to Pose
The animation can be planned using key poses (top, ground impact, rebound), ensuring smooth transitions.

10. Exaggeration
The squash and stretch can be increased beyond realism to make the motion more dynamic.

11. Solid Drawing
The ball maintains consistent volume even when deformed, preserving its 3D appearance.

12. Appeal
Even a simple bouncing ball becomes visually engaging with proper timing and motion.
